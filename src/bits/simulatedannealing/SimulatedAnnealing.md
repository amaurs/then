# Simulated Annealing

Part of the color space collection. Simulated annealing borrows its logic from metallurgy: heat a material until its atoms move freely, then cool it slowly so they settle into a low-energy crystalline structure. This piece applies that metaphor to the arrangement of colors on a canvas, beginning with a random distribution and iteratively swapping color positions, accepting improvements unconditionally and accepting degradations with a probability that decreases over time.

The cooling schedule is the work's hidden parameter — the variable that determines whether the final image resembles a frozen snapshot of chaos or a smoothly organized gradient. Too fast, and the colors lock into a disordered state; too slow, and the computation becomes impractical. The piece occupies the productive middle ground, where the algorithm has had enough time to discover large-scale structure but not enough to eliminate every local imperfection. The result is an image that looks _almost_ organized — a landscape of color that the eye reads as intentional but that retains, at close inspection, the residual disorder of an incomplete optimization.
