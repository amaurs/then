# Hilbert

Part of the color space collection. David Hilbert's space-filling curve, introduced in 1891, is a fractal construction that visits every point in a square without gaps or overlaps — a continuous mapping from one dimension to two. This piece applies that mapping to the RGB color cube, unfolding three dimensions of color into a two-dimensional Hilbert curve that fills the canvas.

The result is a tapestry of over sixteen million colors arranged according to the curve's recursive, self-similar structure. Because the Hilbert curve preserves locality — points that are near each other in one dimension tend to remain near each other in two — the image exhibits smooth, coherent gradients that reflect the underlying geometry of color space. The piece demonstrates that the relationship between dimension and perception is not fixed but negotiable: with the right mapping, a line can become a plane, and a cube can become an image.
